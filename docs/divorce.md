It was the spring of 1996. One sunny weekend day, as I was strolling with my
wife in Lakeshore Park in Toronto, I asked her, for the millionth time, if there
was anything going on between her and our friend Jan.

“What if there was?” was her answer.

“Oh, that’s new,” I thought to myself, “she always denied before.”
“So it is true, after all?” I asked.

“Yes,” she finally admitted.

“Okay, so now what? Where do we go from here?”

“I don’t quite know,” she said, “I still have feelings for you.”

“Well, then, can’t you break up with him and we try to fix our marriage?”

“Unfortunately, no, I don’t think I can,” she replied.

Only later I realized I was well into the classic “five stages of grief.” Having
finished the denial and anger stages, I was entering the bargaining stage. I
actually pleaded with her to stay with me: “Take some time to reflect on this,
please.” I took the Greyhound bus to Hamilton to stay with her sister and her
husband for a few days, in order to give her some space. It didn’t help.

When I came back, she was already packed and ready to move out. We sat down on
the couch in our living room and hugged for a long time. Then she left for good.

I remember I had to pick up something from some Romanian family the next day,
and I was in complete shock. Life was continuing its course but I felt
completely disoriented. Although I had been expecting this for a long time
already, when it finally happened it felt as if I had been hit by a truck. I was
trying really hard not to cry; I was in some stranger’s house, they didn’t know
anything. When I finally got home I got into bed and sobbed, crying myself to
sleep.

I awake the next day with a strange feeling: I was on my own, for the
first time in years. I went for a long walk downtown, and as I was
wandering aimlessly I saw a poster for a David Copperfield magic show. That's
when it hit me: I can just buy a ticket and go, on an impulse, without asking
anybody! So I went. The show exceeded all my expectations: I had seen David
Copperfield on TV many times, but seeing his magic acts up close and personal
was a completely different feeling! Once it was over, I was feeling a lot
better and I started looking for more shows to go to. 

After a few more days, as I was adjusting to my new status, I tried really hard
to see the bright side of this: now that it had happened it was no longer a
looming threat, and it felt easier to bear, somehow. I started thinking of the
positives: I was no longer required to tell anybody what time I’d be home, I
could go and do whatever I wanted, I could meet whomever I wanted, make new
friends and even date again.

That prospect was a bit scary though: aside from Ioana I had never really dated
anyone before, and I was now 29 years old. I had not had a new girlfriend for
the past ten years, in fact. Add to that the small detail that I was in a
foreign country, still relatively a newcomer, with basically no friends and no
family. And what did I know about the dating protocol in Canada anyway?

I started spending a lot of time in bookstores, especially the shiny new
Chapters bookstore on Bloor Street, which had comfortable armchairs one could
sit in for hours. While browsing aimlessly I found the Self-Help section and one
title caught my attention: "Divorce for Beginners: How to Get Unhitched Without
the Hitches", by Cathy Hopkins. It was a cartoon book that used humor to discuss
the practical aspects of getting it together after a divorce. It was just what I
needed! Humor is what I always fall back on, no matter what, and it has never
failed me.

Now that I was feeling a little better I thought to contact my former University
of Bucharest schoolmate Dana, who was doing a PhD in Paris. So I wrote her an
email to let her know Ioana had left me and asked her if we could talk. In 1996
international phone calls were expensive and there was no WhatsApp. But there
was a live chat terminal-based Unix program called “talk,” and it was free. So
Dana wrote back to me and we set up a time when we could both log in and have a
live text session. This was an exciting new development at the time, being able
to text in real time for free over the Atlantic!

So, at the appointed time we started our “talk” session. After a few
pleasantries, I went straight into the heart of the matter:

“Dana, did you know that I had a big crush on you all through
college?” 

“Yes, I know,” she said, “it was hard not to notice, but
what did you expect me to do about it? You were always with Ioana.”

She was right. Even though I was very attracted to her I never dared to tell
her. She seemed completely out of reach for me. She had a boyfriend too. Also, I
liked her so much I was never able to have a relaxed conversation with her; I
just froze in front of her.

What happened later took me by surprise, to say the least. After we
exhausted the subject of my infatuation with her, Dana said out of the
blue:

“Why don’t you come over to Paris for an internship at INRIA? It would
take your mind off of things.”

“How would I do that?” I asked. “I don’t know anyone there.”

“No, but they know you.”

“Huh, you’re kidding, right?”

“No, I’m not. It so happens that your PhD advisor came last month and
gave a talk about your research at INRIA. I was there in the room, and
my advisor asked a lot of questions. I can let him know that you are
available for a possible internship, if you want.”

“Wow, that would be fantastic, thanks Dana!” I said.

A few weeks later I got an official invitation letter from Patrick
Valduriez, and I started booking my flights and accommodation in
Paris. I decided to use this opportunity to also visit my parents in
Bucharest before Paris.

So in the course of a few weeks after Ioana left, I received the
official divorce papers (no-fault divorce with no communal property to
divide), signed them, and was getting ready for my trip to Europe!

Before leaving I went to visit the few Romanian friends I had in
Toronto, asking them if they needed me to take anything to their
parents. One of them, the wife of Razvan (a former schoolmate of Jan,
as it happens), told me he had a girlfriend in Bucharest she could
introduce me to, if I was interested. She even gave me a little
bracelet to take to her, as an excuse to meet her.

I did meet her friend Raluca in Bucharest; we even dated for a few
weeks while I was there, but neither of us took it very
seriously. Still, she was the first girl I dated after my divorce, and
we enjoyed our time together in Mamaia (a beach resort on the Black
Sea coast) and Sinaia (a resort in the Bucegi Mountains). I even
introduced her to my parents. My mom thanked her for helping me get
back on my feet. She was genuinely touched, she confessed later in a
letter I received in Paris. She explained in that letter that she
didn’t want to go too far with me because she was certain I would have
felt obligated to ask her to marry me, and she didn’t think we had
enough in common. It’s true that I had internalized my mom’s advice
she gave me as a teenager to “Never date any girl you can’t see
yourself wanting to marry!”, but in this case I wasn’t really all that
taken with her (even though I was eager to find love again, fresh
divorcee as I was—but more on that later).
