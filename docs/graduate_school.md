I enrolled in graduate school in September 1994. At U of T I learned
about compilers, scientific computing, databases, human-computer
interaction, and theory of computation. My MSc thesis research was
about making the Web accessible to applications through an SQL-like
abstraction, essentially treating it as a standard database, much like
the databases I had previously used in my earlier jobs. Although my
proposed architecture was not adopted by the industry as such, some
similar ideas did resurface later.

In the summers of 1996 and 1997 I worked as a summer intern at INRIA,
near Versailles, in France, not writing any code, but designing a
framework for registering, discovering, and querying structured data
sources on the Web, which I later incorporated in my PhD thesis. In
the summer of 1999 I had another internship, this time in Palo Alto,
at the HP-Compaq Systems Research Center. There, I implemented a mini
Web search engine, called Hilltop, which computed page relevance
scores by aggregating links from non-affiliated websites. Even though
it relied on a tiny memory-resident index, it worked surprisingly well
for broad topics. In addition to working with our mentors, the
internship gave me and the other students the opportunity to meet with
some of the full-time researchers at SRC who were happy to discuss
their research with us, in one-on-one sessions. This is how I met
Leslie Lamport (best known for LaTeX), Mike Burrows, Andrei Broder,
and other outstanding computer scientists who were working on an array
of exciting projects: Web search engines, portable music players,
pocket computers, and other innovations that were dismissed by the HP
corporation at the time only to become huge commercial successes years
later for other companies.

Back from a great summer, I got back to my PhD thesis with renewed
energy, eager to graduate and go work on exciting stuff in the real
world. I decided then that I didn’t want to look for a job in
academia. So I started looking for jobs at industrial research labs
and other software companies, all in the United States, as it happens.
