
As I neared the end of my PhD at the University of Toronto, I started thinking
seriously about what would come next. The path into the job market for someone
finishing a doctorate in database research at the time ran through a single
mailing list. As I was scheduling my thesis defense, I heard from other students
in our research group about dbGrads, part of a SIGMOD-run system called dbWorld
that connected graduating database researchers with academic and industry
opportunities. I registered without thinking much of it. Then one day, out of
nowhere, I got an email from a hiring manager at the IBM Thomas J. Watson
Research Center in Hawthorne, New York, inviting me for an interview.

I went, gave a talk on my dissertation work, and met with a handful of research
staff members and research engineers, all reporting to the same manager. One of
the people I met with was Stu Feldman, who turned out to be Vice President for
Computer Science across all of IBM Research, though I only half-grasped at the
time what that meant. He was also, as I’d later learn, one of the people who had
helped build Unix itself at Bell Labs, had written the first Fortran 77
compiler, and had created make. None of that came up in the room. He was
remarkably relaxed about the whole thing. “I’m not going to make you jump
through any more hoops,” he told me. “The other interviewers already did
that. I’m just here to tell you what the expectations are like here: we
understand that you will have your strong years and your weaker years, and you
will not be penalized for that as long as you keep doing good work.”

During my internship at the Systems Research Center in Palo Alto in 1999, I had
worked with a researcher named Krishna Bharat on something we called the Hilltop
algorithm, a way of identifying authoritative pages on the web for the purposes
of ranking search results. I remember seeing a cloth banner with Google’s logo
hanging from one of the buildings on University Avenue that summer, back when
the name still meant nothing to most people. A group of Google people invited
Krishna out for lunch while I was still his intern, and not long after, he
announced his resignation from SRC. He timed his departure deliberately, staying
just long enough for us to finish the work and for me to give my final
internship talk. He even helped me polish the PowerPoint presentation. His last
day at SRC was the same day as mine.

Krishna joined Google that fall, when the company was still tiny. By the spring
of 2000 I already had my offer in hand from IBM Research, the job I had actually
wanted all along, and was finishing up my PhD. Then, in the early summer,
Krishna invited me out for an interview at Google for a Research Scientist
position. I flew out and presented my actual PhD thesis work, on publishing,
locating, and querying networked information sources, rather than Hilltop, which
would have interested them far more. I didn’t know it at the time, but Google
was already in the process of acquiring the Hilltop patent from HP, the parent
company of SRC.

After the interview I flew back to Romania for a visit. While I was there, an
email arrived from Google asking me to come back for another round, a full day
of back-to-back coding and systems design interviews. They paid for the trip. I
remember one interviewer in particular, Urs Hölzle (who would go on to become
Google’s eighth employee of record and eventually Senior Vice President of
Technical Infrastructure, one of the most senior engineers in the company’s
history), who asked me to choose between working on Search or working on
Ads. When it was all over, I received an offer, for a Software Engineer
position, a role I hadn’t even realized I was being considered for. I had been
interviewing the whole time for Research Scientist.

With the IBM offer already in hand, I could compare them directly. Google’s cash
offer was lower than IBM’s, and the Bay Area cost of living was considerably
higher than Westchester County. To compensate, the offer from Google came with
twenty thousand stock options, priced at eighty-five cents a share. At the time,
those options meant nothing to me. There was no IPO on the horizon, not for
years, and I had just spent years earning a doctorate. So I turned it down,
choosing IBM Research instead. I'll let you do the math what those stock options
would have been worth when Google finally went public.

Soon after, I finished my PhD. My scholarship money ran out the moment I
graduated, and so did something else: my apartment. I had been living in
Graduate Family Housing on Charles Street with my fiancee, Grațiela, and
graduation meant we had to be out by the end of September. We put our furniture
into storage and went looking for somewhere to live while my US work visa, the
one for IBM, was still being processed with no end in sight. After a lot of
searching we found a furnished studio on Isabella Street for $1,500 a month,
nearly triple what we had been paying as students. We had no income to speak of,
only Grațiela’s PhD stipend, but we had no real choice, so we took it and
started dipping into my credit cards to cover the difference.

The visa process was no faster then than it is now. There was exactly one path
that could get someone across the border quickly: a NAFTA visa, available to
citizens of Canada working in a defined list of professions. Software engineer
was on that list. Computer science researcher was not. My job at IBM had been
classified as research, which meant the fast path wasn’t available to me, no
matter how qualified I was. It reminded me, in a way, of the Canadian
immigration officer years before who had rejected my application the moment I
called myself a computer scientist, and approved it the moment I said I could
also work as a software engineer.

With money running out and no end in sight, I started looking for backup
options, specifically jobs classified as software engineering rather than
research, since those could get me into the country immediately. I wrote to all
the people I knew in the States, and to my surprise, they all came through. You
see, we were at the height of the dot-com boom, and everybody was a founder:
Anthony Tomasic had started a company to build a system for automatically
generating grocery orders from cooking recipes and wanted to connect that with
Martha Steward's TV show, Alon Halevy just founded Nimble Technologies to
develop data integration solutions, and Dana was working for Adam Bosworth, who
had left Microsoft with his team to found Crossgain. I wasn't sold on Anthony's
business idea, but I had a lot of respect for Alon and I trusted Dana's
intuition. Alon, who was the external examiner for my PhD thesis invited me
right away for an interview at Nimble. Dana told her boss about me and he called
me from cell phone from his morning run the next day: he explained to me his
vision for building Web applications from simple, generic components that can be
deployed on different servers and would talk to each other using an XML-based
protocol. I was intrigued by this idea, remember, this was in early 2000, before
anybody heard of Roy Fielding's thesis that formalized REST.

It so happened that both start-ups were in Seattle so they could share the
travel expenses for my interviews. So I got on a plane and flew to Seattle, all
expenses paid. First, I interviewed at Crossgain: they had me meet with several
engineers and code on the whiteboard, then Adam invited me in his office to
offer me a software engineer job. I told him I already have an offer for a research
position from IBM but I took his written offer and told him I'll give him an
answer soon.

After the Crossgain interview I still had a day before the Nimble interview and
I decided to drive to Vancouver for the day. Once there I took the ferry to
Victoria Island. It was a great trip, but I got back to Seattle after
midnight. I was wiped the next day at the interview, which may explain why I
didn't get any offer from Nimble.


I told my hiring manager at IBM Research what was happening: that I had an offer
in hand elsewhere, that I intended to take it on a temporary basis just to get
into the country, and that I planned to come back to IBM once my actual visa was
sorted out.

That got their attention. Within a day or two, IBM had made a few calls to their
immigration lawyers and come back with a solution: they would issue me a new
offer letter, identical in salary and responsibilities to the one I already had,
but with one change. My title would read “Software Engineer” instead of
“Researcher.” On paper, nothing about my actual work would change. On paper was
exactly what mattered. I drove to the border with the new letter in December,
and the visa was issued on the spot.  I worked as a “software engineer” for the
first few months, doing exactly the research I had been hired to do, until the
paperwork caught up and they quietly switched my title back. It was absurd, and
it was also exactly the kind of absurdity that, after Romania, didn’t strike me
as particularly strange.

Once the visa finally came through, Grațiela and I got married at City Hall. I
drove down to White Plains alone to start the job. She followed by train a few
weeks later, and nearly didn’t make it across the border: when the customs
officer asked the purpose of her visit and she said she was going to see her
husband, the officer told her flatly that visiting a husband was not what a
tourist visa was for, that she needed a proper dependent visa instead. Somehow,
she took pity on her, and let her through on the promise that she would return
to Toronto after the holidays and apply for the right paperwork.

I was waiting for her at Pennsylvania Station, in the middle of the crowd, and
we missed each other completely. I ended up having her name called out over the
public address system before we finally found one another. I brought her home to
our new apartment, a two-bedroom on Lake Street in White Plains.

I have to say, after living in Toronto for seven years, I found White Plains
quite dull. There really wasn't much to do, especially in the cold winter, other
than hanging out in the Westchester Mall, the only place we could walk around
and not freeze. Seeing the prices in the stores there got me thinking my large
looking IBM salary wasn't really all that much. Since we were just hastily
married in City Hall before leaving Toronto, we started planning a wedding
ceremony in church, and we were in the market for a wedding dress. We started
looking at bridal catalogs and everything was in the thousands of dollars. The
only affordable items were something called "HP", which were only a few
hundreds. "Let's get one of these", Gratiela was saying, happy that she found
something we could afford. When we looked more carefully we figured out what
"HP" meant: "Head Piece". So not a dress. Oh, well, in the end we did find a
very nice cream-white dress at Macy's, not technically a wedding gown, but it
did the job.

After the New Year's day, 2001, Gratiela was due back to Toronto, for her
doctoral research and also to get a proper dependent visa. She only stayed a
couple weeks there and came back, after arranging with her thesis advisor that
she will continue working remotely and meet once per quarter. So a few months
passed uneventfully until one day Gratiela started feeling sick and we went to
see a doctor at the local clinic. After he consulted her, he came looking for me
in the waiting room:

"Mr. Mihaila?", he called.

"Yes", I said, getting up, not knowing what to expect.

"Well, sir, I consulted your wife, and everything is fine, no need to worry."

"Oh, so what was it?", I asked, relieved.

"She's expecting! You're going to have a baby, she's due around November 1st."

"Wow! Thank you!", I blurted, taken by surprise.

"Congratulations to both of you!", said the doctor, and left.

Gratiela came out right after, in a daze. It took us a bit of time to process
the news: we were extremely happy, of course, even though we didn't "plan for
it". What that meant, though, was that for our church wedding service had to
scheduled sooner than we were initially thinking, and her cream-white dress from
Macy's was going to be a little tight. No matter, though.

We had a lovely wedding service, with Laura and her husband Serghi as
god-parents. Yes, the same Laura that introduced us, back in Toronto. They flew
to New York for a few days, and we had a very small party with a couple of other
Romanian friends from New York City. Our parents could not attend, sadly, there
was no time for them to get tourist visas.

We started going to the regular checkups and eventually to a sonogram where the
radiologist showed us our baby's feet on the computer screen, singing "These
feet are made for walking, and that's just what they'll do, one of these days
these feet are gonna walk right over you!". Talk about foreshadowing!

Everything was going smoothly, my new job at IBM Research was interesting,
feeling very much like the work I was doing in graduate school, not necessarily
content-wise, more like effort-wise: it was all quite laid back, with no
deadlines to speak of, other than conference submission deadlines. In the
weekends we would escape the White Plains boredom by taking trips to the city,
walking in Central Park, and all the touristy places. One such Sunday in early
September we went to visit the World Trade Center and took the South Tower
elevator to the top floor and strolled on the terrace, taking in the view. The
next Tuesday morning we woke up as usual, had breakfast, and I got in my car to
go to my office in Hawthorne. I was about half way there, when I turned on the
radio; instead of the usual classical music, though, all I could hear was a live
report from the city, a terrible accident had just happened, the reporters were
saying, a commercial plane had hit the North Tower. I continued driving to
work. Sixteen minutes later, though, the South Tower was hit by a second
plane. At this point, it became clear this wasn't an accident. I parked my car
and went straight to my manager's office. He was oblivious to all of that. I
told him what was happening, and without hesitation he told me to go home to
comfort my expecting wife, she was less than two months away from her due date
at that point. So I did. When I got home, Gratiela was watching the news on TV,
unable to believe her eyes. For the next few hours we called our parents to tell
them we're OK, and then called all our friends from the city to make sure they
are safe, offering to host them in our apartment if needed. Thankfully, they
were all living far enough from Ground Zero, as the former site of the World
Trade Center became known.

Just a few weeks before we had contacted a real estate agent to help us find a
home in Westchester County. When she called us afterwards we told her we're
canceling our search for now, we really didn't know what was going to happen to
America. Nothing was the same after that, newspapers were full of hate for all
Muslims, huge Wanted pictures of Osama bin Laden on their front pages, airport
security became draconic, immigration cases were subjected to heightened
scrutiny. Luckily, my H1B visa was already issued, but the green card process
ended up taking close to five years.

Slowly things got back to some resemblance of normal, and on November 4th we
went to the White Plains Hospital and our daughter was born. The first card on
her crib said "Baby Girl Mihaila", and only later they asked her what we were
going to call her. "Maria", we said, "no middle name". As Gratiela had developed
a slight fever during the lengthy delivery, the doctor took Maria to the ICU as
a precaution and stuck a bunch of needles and sensors into her tiny body. They
discharged Gratiela, though, so we went home baby-less. Gratiela's mother had
already arrived from Romania, to help with the baby. She was terribly
disappointed when she saw us, and so were we, although we were mostly exhausted
from the sleepless night spent at the hospital. After a couple of days though we
were finally allowed to take Maria home! We were overjoyed, but also terrified,
there she was, a tiny human, entirely dependent on us.
