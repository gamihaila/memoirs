The first night back in Bucharest I was completely wiped, the long flight and the seven-hour time difference catching up to me all at once. My head was spinning and I was in a foul mood, seriously questioning why I had even made the trip. This was the same trip during which Google had called me back to meet with more people, so it wasn’t as if I had nothing to occupy my mind. I barely slept that first night, and woke up far too early, still jet-lagged, so I went out for a walk alone in Cișmigiu Garden to try to clear my head. There was a lot to clear: my ruined relationships, the Google interview that hadn’t yet led anywhere, the IBM offer with its clock quietly ticking, an offer from AT&T Research I hadn’t even mentioned to anyone because I had no real interest in living in New Jersey, my whole future suspended in midair. Most of all, though, I felt terrible about the suffering I had caused Grațiela, who hadn’t deserved any of it. But I had promised not to call her, so I didn’t. I went home that evening with nothing resolved.

A few more days passed the same way. Then one evening, as I sat staring at the walls of my childhood bedroom, lit by nothing but a single weak bulb in the ceiling fixture, the phone rang. My father picked it up. “It’s for you,” he called out. I took the cordless phone into my room and answered. It was Grațiela, calling from a beer garden next to the Bucharest Opera House, where she was drinking with her old dorm roommate and best friend, Oana. “I’m here with my best friend,” she said, “and she insists she wants to meet the man who ruined my life, and give him a piece of her mind.”

“Coming right up,” I said, without a second of hesitation.

I got into a cab and twenty minutes later I was sitting with the two of them, drinking what Oana liked to call “melancholic beverages.” Whatever awkwardness I had braced for melted away almost immediately, Oana scolded me thoroughly over a few beers, and Grațiela was laughing again, relaxed in a way I hadn’t seen in weeks. We were back together before the night was over. In fact, we never went to sleep at all. We visited a friend in a nearby dormitory and stayed up until the sun came up, then took a long walk through the old downtown as the city woke up around us.

The next day I brought her to meet my parents properly. My mother threw together a feast, my sister came too, and it was wonderful. The day after that, Grațiela and I went to a resort on the Black Sea and spent a few lovely days there. One afternoon, strolling along the beach, we noticed an Internet cafe and ducked in to check our email. The timing turned out to matter more than I expected: in my University of Toronto inbox was a message from the graduate coordinator asking me to submit my dissertation abstract by the end of the week. Thanks to Telnet, available at this small Black Sea internet cafe of all places, I logged into the university’s main Unix server, qew.cs.toronto.edu, named for the Queen Elizabeth Way along the Toronto shoreline, and submitted my abstract just in time.

We made our way back to Bucharest, where I received the email from Google asking me to come back for the second round of interviews. I didn’t want to cut the vacation short, so I scheduled it for right after we were due back in Toronto anyway.

As soon as we landed, I called Lara to tell her it was really over this time. She came by our apartment on Charles Street to collect her remaining things, including, of course, Ioana’s old nightgowns. I met her in the lobby downstairs. We hugged for a long time, and then she was gone, and seemed, finally, at peace with it.

A few weeks later, a man called me wanting to meet and talk about Lara. We sat down at a coffee shop next to my building, and I did my best to paint an honest, generous picture of her: how beautifully she played Debussy on the piano, what a kind-hearted person she was underneath everything, all of it true, leaving out the rest. “If she’s so wonderful,” he asked, somewhat reasonably, “how come you’re no longer together?” “Oh,” I told him, “she just wasn’t all that into me, I’m afraid.” I wished him well and meant it.
