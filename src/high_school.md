The high school I had earned my way into with that near-disastrous Romanian exam score was, though I didn’t know it at the time, one of the most storied schools in the country. It went by the name Liceul Nicolae Bălcescu, but its real identity was older and grander than that. Founded in 1694 as the Royal Academy of Bucharest, it had been known for most of its history as Colegiul Național Sfântul Sava, and after 1992 it would reclaim that name. The teachers were old-school intellectuals in the truest sense — rigorous, cultured, and demanding in a way that felt less like pedagogy and more like an initiation into a long tradition of serious thought.

My first encounter with Computer Science was in high school, in my junior year, if I’m not mistaken, so around 1983. It was during a class called Mathematics Applied to Computing Technology. It was there that I first learned about flowcharts as a way of designing an algorithm. I remember what attracted me was the possibility of teaching a machine how to solve math homework problems like finding the solutions of a quadratic equation: how to systematically treat each case and always find the solutions or determine that no solution existed. This was one of the types of exercises I had to do so many times for homework in the past, and it was great to see an algorithm that automatically solved this problem for all possible cases.

Later, I remember, we were given an assignment to come up with an algorithm for sorting a list of numbers, without actually being shown any in class. Needless to say, my “algorithm” was a complete mess that probably didn’t do the job, but it was so convoluted that it was hard for anyone to reason their way through it and decide what it did! After struggling through this assignment on our own, we were of course shown a few of the easy and inefficient solutions, and I believe we even proved their correctness formally, with invariants, using induction!

I remember that before this class, I was quite unsure which path to take, oscillating between electronics, theoretical physics, and pure mathematics. The electronics path was quickly dismissed after I tried (and failed) to build a radio receiver from a kit. In my defense, the instructions were in Russian, but still. Theoretical physics would probably involve working at a nuclear reactor research facility, and I was not sure how safe that was. So pure mathematics stayed as an option, but I didn’t see myself becoming a professional mathematician and I wasn’t fond of teaching.

After this Applied Math class, though, my interest was piqued and I started seeing how I could maybe pursue a degree in computing. At the time, there were three options for computing degrees in Romania: Electronics and Automatics (in the Polytechnics University) — with a heavy focus on hardware; Cybernetics (in the Economics Academy); and Mathematics-Informatics (in the Mathematics department of the University of Bucharest). Out of the three, I felt closest to Informatics: as the radio receiver fiasco showed, I should stay away from hardware; I despised Economics; and I liked Math. Plus, I was reasoning that mistakes in programming can always be fixed, unlike frying electronic components.

Around the same time, I had another obsession running in parallel: photography. I had an old Russian camera, and what fascinated me was the developing process. My father had a Soviet-made enlarger sitting around, and I claimed it along with the only room in the apartment that could be made completely dark: the bathroom. Black and white materials were all I worked with; the paper and chemicals were affordable and easy to find in stores. Developing the film, letting it dry, then projecting the image on photographic paper, watching it appear in the developer tray. It was fascinating.

My subjects were mostly schoolmates, especially the girls in our class. I was handing out prints for free until our homeroom teacher noticed and suggested I charge enough to cover my costs. It had not occurred to me, but sure, why not. I set the price at one leu per print, roughly twenty-five cents today. Not much, but it added up. I still have many of those prints in a box somewhere.

I did not feel bad about charging them. They were mostly from well-off families, already on track for Medical School or Law School. My own family was comfortable enough on paper; my father was a university professor and my mother an editor at a prestigious publishing house. But my father liked to live well. He enjoyed taking fellow professors out to dinner, especially at Casa Universitarilor, the faculty restaurant. 

Money had a way of slipping through my father’s fingers, which was saying something in an economy where there was precious little to spend it on. The stores were largely empty. If you wanted a color television, you put your name on a waiting list and came back in five or six years. A car meant paying the full price upfront, roughly three years’ salary, into an escrow account you couldn’t touch, and then waiting another three or four years for the vehicle to be manufactured and delivered. It was a pretty bad car to begin with, but it was the only one available. Refrigerators, washing machines, anything of value moved the same way: through connections, through someone who knew someone at the factory. The classified ads in the back of the newspapers filled the rest of the gap, chock full of VCRs and electronics that relatives abroad had sent by mail and that were now being resold for many times their Western price.

The regime’s explanation for all this was taught to us as self-evident truth. We were gloriously building a great socialist economy centered around industrial manufacturing, producing goods destined for foreign commerce, bringing in the hard currency needed to pay off the national debt ahead of schedule. Domestic consumption was an afterthought. Capitalism, meanwhile, was woefully wasteful and morally bankrupt, its huge income inequality a symptom of a system in terminal decline. We were supposed to feel fortunate. We looked at the empty shelves and did our best.

What the regime could not quite reconcile was its own television programming. Western movies and shows were broadcast on Romanian national TV, chosen by the censors because they depicted crime, moral corruption, and the spiritual emptiness of capitalist life. Dallas ran for multiple seasons, an oil dynasty rotting from within, wives driven to alcoholism by lives of idle luxury. The intended lesson was clear. What we actually absorbed was that people in the West lived in enormous houses, drove beautiful cars, and had swimming pools. The censors had miscalculated, as censors often do.

And yet there were things that were genuinely good, and chief among them was the cultural life of Bucharest. The city had a dozen theaters offering live productions every night, with actors and directors of real caliber performing translations of international literature alongside the Romanian classics. There was the Philharmonic, there was the Opera House, and the standard at both was serious and high. Radio was perhaps the greatest surprise to anyone discovering it from the outside: not just classical music, but full theatrical productions with professional casts, sound design, real production value. Thousands of these radio plays existed in the archives, and they were unlike anything I later encountered in the West. Audiobooks were a pale substitute; these were something else entirely, a complete art form that happened to be invisible.

None of this was accidental. The regime understood that culture was useful, a pressure valve and a source of national pride, and it funded these institutions generously. But the artists and intellectuals who filled them brought something the regime hadn’t bargained for: genuine excellence, and a tradition of serious thought that no ideological overlay could entirely suppress. My father was part of that world. A linguist and philologist whose research into the cultural history of Romania and its Slavic influences would eventually earn him full membership in the Romanian Academy, he carried his intellectual life into everything he did. Dinner conversations ranged across history, etymology, and the cultural crosscurrents between Romania and its neighbors. On our Sunday walks through Bucharest he would talk about the city’s past, its layers of influence, the stories embedded in the names of streets and buildings. It was an education that no classroom could have provided, and I didn’t fully appreciate it until much later.
