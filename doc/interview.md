Ileana: Walk me through a completely normal day when you
were like eight years old. Start from waking up.

George: Eight years old, waking up — so that must be
something around like third grade. So I would wake up.
Actually, I wouldn't wake up by myself — my dad would wake
me up, most likely in a very gentle way. They would wake up
Puși; I think Puși should have been woken up earlier. So at
eight years old, meaning third grade, I probably had school
in the afternoon. Yes, because our school was so overcrowded
that they had to split the classes by year: first and second
grade were in the morning, third grade was in the afternoon,
and then fourth grade in the afternoon, something like that.
Anyway, I must have been in the afternoon, so that would
mean I had plenty of time to have breakfast without being
rushed.

My dad would make me breakfast, and then I would get started
on my homework.

I would work on my homework, maybe have enough time to read
or waste time, and then lunchtime would come — because
classes would only start at one o'clock, I had lunchtime.
But keep in mind I was all by myself, because my parents
were both working. So my mom bought me an electric heater
for food, like an electric stove-top thing, and she would
leave soup for me, and something like mashed potatoes and a
chicken leg, or something like that, for me to heat up.

Now, I liked the mashed potatoes and the chicken leg, but I
did not like the soup. So what I would do — because I didn't
want my mom to know that I didn't eat the soup — I would
pour the soup into a plate, get it dirty really well with a
spoon, and then I would dump it in the toilet. I used to do
that — dump it in the toilet — so it looked like I ate,
because I wouldn't wash dishes anyway. So I'd keep the plate
on the table, so it looked like I ate. Then I would heat up
the mashed potatoes and the chicken leg, which was not easy,
because they would stick to the pan; I had to stir it a
little bit. Anyway, I would eat it, and then I would get
dressed in my uniform — because we had uniforms — put my
shoes on, and walk to school. Now, if it was springtime,
that would mean there was a lot of mud around my home. We
lived in an apartment building, and I had to walk about 10
minutes to school.

There was a lot of mud, because... I guess they were
constructing something, building some new homes, and it was
very muddy. So I would put plastic bags around my shoes,
wrap my shoes in plastic bags, and walk to school. Then when
I got out of the muddy area, I would take the garbage bags
off, throw them in the garbage, and have my clean shoes so I
could go into the school building.

Get into the school building. There was usually an assembly
in the morning to count people. We'd line up by class — we
couldn't just go into the class — and we'd gather together
in the schoolyard. Then I think we would say some kind of —
not Pledge of Allegiance, but some kind of patriotic
something, an anthem probably. And then we would go into
class.

Ileana: Okay, let's see. Speaking of childhood — were you
aware of any kind of... like, okay. Were you aware of, you
know, the totalitarian regime?

George: Oh, totally.

Ileana: And what was that like, to be aware of it? Were you
upset about it, or were you just like, "ah, whatever"?

George: So we were trained to essentially have a double
life. We would talk against the regime — my parents would
tell me how horrible it was, privately at home — and they
would always criticize the personality cult. We used to
point at the actors on TV who would sing praises to the
leader and comment about how ridiculous that was, and how it
was getting worse and worse every year, how these phrases
were completely out of line with reality.

Ileana: Do you think you see common kinds of fake
nationalism now in America — how preposterous the government
is getting, and how people are kind of praising some...

George: Not really, because here people have different
opinions, and they can openly criticize and express their
opinions. But back there, you could only do that within the
confines of your own home. The moment you got out of the
house, you had to pretend everything was nice and peachy,
that you liked your leader, you loved your leader, and you
were grateful for the free education and free textbooks and
free everything. Suddenly in school you couldn't say
anything. Now, kids would sometimes be critical, talk a
little bit about how bad things were, but not that much,
because we were really afraid of each other. Anybody could
be a tattletale.

Ileana: Was it a real fear that someone would tattle and
your family would get...

George: I don't know about the family, but I could
personally get in trouble.

Ileana: Did you know anyone who got in trouble?

George: I didn't know anybody who got in trouble, but I had
one kid — this was later, not when I was eight, a little
later — who threatened me that he would tell the teachers
about how I expressed my views against the leader. He would
use this to blackmail me, and I didn't know if he was
serious or not, but I didn't want to take any chances.

Yeah, so nothing.

Ileana: Well, we don't have to talk about age eight anymore.
But like, generally, I remember you telling me about the TV
channels, and how there's very limited...

George: I mean, now that we're talking about totalitarianism
and whether I was scared or not, I do recall one incident.
Somebody broke something in the bathroom at school — nothing
to do with politics at all — but they got some investigators
into the school to round up some people at random, and I was
one of the people rounded up to see if anybody knew anything
about who did it. It was not political, but that was
extremely scary. Those guys were telling us about how we
could lose our freedom, how we could get locked up, how we
could get into prison for failing to talk about something —
if we knew who did it and we didn't tell, we could get in
trouble.

Yeah, I was terrified. I mean, I was one of the good kids; I
never got in trouble, but they rounded me up at random.
Anyway, they were trying to get into our heads that this is
serious, and we could actually go to jail.

Ileana: Do you think they were just using that incident to
scare you guys about keeping secrets from the government
later on? Like, they didn't actually care about...

George: No, I don't think they actually cared about the
bathroom. It was just to scare us. That's how I felt —
knowing what I know about the political persecution, I took
it as a warning that, hey, if you think you are fine, you're
actually not, because at any moment you could be taken to
jail for something you did or didn't do — it doesn't
actually matter.

It was a terrifying moment.

They eventually let us go, and nothing happened, but they
made sure to drill into us that if they wanted to put us in
jail, they could, and nobody could stop them.

Ileana: Well, yeah. See, I have some other questions. That's
crazy, though. Yeah. I think this must have been something
like 6th or 7th grade.

So — what were you taught about the West, and what did you
actually think?

George: That's very interesting, because we would be told
that Western society was on the brink of collapse — moral
collapse — and that there were people on the streets,
beggars. I mean, it's true, the homeless. But they were
essentially making it sound like society was on the brink of
collapse, and it was only a matter of time until it would
disintegrate. But at the same time they were showing movies
and TV shows from America on our national TV that showed
life was pretty darn luxurious and nice.

Ileana: Okay, so I learned about some kind of CIA program —
America had something called Free Europe or something —
where America could broadcast certain things beyond the Iron
Curtain. Was that how you watched and listened to Western
things, or was it just allowed?

George: No, that was actually on Romanian national TV. They
were showing — I guess there was always some kind of
problem, right? Movies usually have problems, like crime,
gangsters, or whatever, and I guess the censorship was
satisfied that that would show us how bad it is in the West.
But what we saw was fancy cars and people living in nice
mansions and having swimming pools. They were supposed to
portray how empty life was and how miserable people were,
because they had no moral compass. For example, Dallas —
Dallas was this TV show, with multiple seasons, that was
supposed to show how corrupt an oil-owning family was, and
how miserable their wives were, turning to alcoholism
because they had nothing to do. But all we could see was
what a good life they had, how rich they were, which was
very strange.

Ileana: So do you think people naturally just have a
tendency towards wanting material goods? Like, okay, well,
duh. But did they teach you Marxism and try to drill that
into you, that after capitalism there has to be a revolution
and socialism?

George: Of course. Yes.

Ileana: Did anyone agree with that?

George: You had to pretend you agreed. It was one of the
subjects we had to learn in school, like Marxist economy. We
were supposed to accept as a self-evident truth that a
planned economy has to be more efficient than market
capitalism. Market capitalism was not planned and would be
very wasteful, because there would be all these companies
that try to compete with each other, and some would lose and
some would win, but all the losers would have spent a lot of
money to produce goods that they cannot sell, and therefore
it's wasteful. As opposed to a planned socialist economy,
where the state would decide exactly how much needs to be
produced of every good, and nothing would be wasted, and
everything would be planned, for five, ten years ahead, and
nothing would be produced for no reason. Everything that
gets produced will be consumed. So we didn't actually
believe that, because we could see that our stores were
empty. We couldn't find anything. We had to put our names on
lists to buy a color TV, and your turn would come in like
five or six years to buy a color TV, because they could only
produce so many every year, and demand was too high. But we
were supposed to believe that that's the way it should be,
because it's planned, and nothing gets produced that will
just stay in stores and nobody would buy it. Same thing with
cars. The only cars available were Romanian-made cars, and
in order to buy a car, you had to pay the price of the car
up front, put it into essentially an escrow account where
you couldn't touch the money, and once you put the money
there, in three or four years your car would be manufactured
and delivered to you — after your money was already sitting
there. The price was about what a regular person would earn
in three years of salary — about 70,000 lei, where somebody
would normally make 2,000 or 3,000 a month. It was an
exorbitant price, but people would do it. People would
scrimp and save and borrow and put the money into this
account, not touch it for five years, and then they would
get the car delivered, which was a pretty bad car to begin
with. But that was the only thing you could buy.

So we were supposed to believe that's the benefit of having
a planned economy, but we could see with our own eyes that
it really wasn't great. This was just one example, but
everything was like that. You wanted to buy a refrigerator?
You couldn't buy a refrigerator. You had to, through
connections, talk to somebody from the factory to get you
ahead of the line and sell you a refrigerator. The other
loophole — the big, big loophole for anything that was not
available — was the classified ads in newspapers. Say
somebody wanted a TV or a VCR. VCRs were a big thing. You
couldn't go to the store and buy a VCR, because there was no
VCR available, but the newspapers were chock full of
classified ads for people selling VCRs. Where would these
VCRs come from?

Well, people would either smuggle them into the country or
have relatives from abroad — usually Israel or some other
places in Western Europe — send them these VCRs by mail.
They would have to pay customs for it, of course, and then
they would put them up for sale, sometimes with brand-new
unopened boxes. You would buy them for a lot more than the
nominal price was in the West, and this was a way for people
who had relatives abroad to make some money. Honestly, they
didn't need the VCRs; maybe they had one for themselves, but
they would get three or four and sell them, and that would
be enough money for, I guess, six months of salary.

This is how I bought my first computer. My first computer
was sold in the dorms, in the university dorm, for something
like 15,000 lei — I remember the price exactly. Again,
salaries were like 3,000 a month.

Now, 15,000 lei — I didn't have that much money. I borrowed
the money from an aunt. An aunt had money saved up for
retirement or whatever; she lent it to me, and my parents
paid her back in installments over a year.

Well, yeah, and that's how I could buy it. The 15,000 lei
would be essentially like $15,000 in purchasing power right
now. You know how much that computer actually cost when the
person who smuggled it into the country bought it? It was
$100. Those computers were made and sold for $100 in Western
Europe, smuggled into Romania, and sold for 15,000. The
exchange rate was that bad.

Ileana: Did you even keep that computer for that long? Like,
long enough to be worth $15,000?

George: Yes — it was the best thing I ever owned. It was
very good, very good deal. I had it from third year of
university; I think I used it for about five years.

Ileana: Okay, okay, I've already asked these... Okay, so
we've talked about different discriminations among ethnic
groups. Was there any class division that you could notice
as a kid? Because I know that since you were in a major
city, it was much different for you than it was for mom —
like with the water and electricity getting turned off.

George: Yes, but that was not something — I mean, I was
aware of it. I was aware that electricity was being rationed
outside of the capital.

Ileana: Now, what are your thoughts on it?

George: I mean, the regime really wanted to save money. I
heard that they paid off all the national debt — Ceaușescu
made it his life mission to repay the entire national debt
in a few years. For that, whatever was produced in Romania
that had any value, he would just export it and not import
anything. That's why you couldn't get a lot of things. Other
communist countries could get things and have it relatively
okay. In fact, other communist countries around us were
better off — Yugoslavia was better off, Poland was better
off. We could see them in summer vacation at the beach; they
would come and sell stuff on the beach to make some money,
like perfumes and cosmetics, because those things were not
available in Romania but were available there, and so they
would make some money that way.

I know that the first time I went out of the country, after
high school, we went to Bulgaria, and I could see that
Bulgarian stores already had a lot more stuff than we had,
and better quality stuff. For example, I could buy records
with Rod Stewart.

They were not available in Romania. We could not buy any
record with any music that's not Romanian music in a store,
and I was surprised. Like, how come Bulgaria has Western
music in the state stores? Also, they had way better
highways. The freeway system was very modern, and to this
day Romania has really bad roads. So Romania was clearly
worse off than a lot of the surrounding countries, but the
debt was paid off.

Ileana: Was there anything that was not bad?

George: Culture was not bad at all. The cultural life was
vibrant. In Bucharest we had at least 10 or 15 theaters with
live plays, amazing actors and amazing productions happening
every single day. You had a wide variety of plays — you
could go see translations from international literature and
Romanian literature. There was the Philharmonic, there was
the Opera House. That was top-notch. That was one thing. The
other thing, which was really good, was education — it was
actually quite good, and it was free all the way, including
college. And healthcare — healthcare was also free. Well,
here's the thing: healthcare was free theoretically, but if
you actually ended up in the hospital and had any kind of
procedure done, any kind of surgery, you were expected to
bribe all the doctors and all the nurses if you wanted
anything. If you wanted good care, you were expected to
bribe.

Ileana: Do you think this is why they don't have a big
tipping culture anymore? Because they don't want people to
think they're asking for bribes? Like in Romania, the
waitresses are so surprised when you tip them.

George: No, it's way more. The doctors were not tips, they
were actual bribes. They were really sizable amounts of
money that you had to pay, and it was kind of understood how
much each procedure was supposed to cost you — in the
thousands and thousands of lei for anything. So yes, it was
free, but it was not really free.

But education was good, culture was good, radio and TV — I
mean, radio was amazing. TV was only a few hours a day of
programming, and that was mostly praise to the party and to
the leaders, except for the American movies that you would
see. But radio was good. We had good classical music, and
also radio plays — theater on the radio.

I haven't seen that in any other country, really. Like
produced, not like audiobooks, but actual actors with roles
and parts and real production value. There are thousands of
those that are still available, recordings, amazing stuff.
So those were good.

What else was good? Outside of the propaganda, it was
understood that everybody knew there were things you
shouldn't criticize openly. Outside of that, life was pretty
normal. I can't say — yes, there were shortages, food
shortages, but people found ways. Everybody would have
relatives in the countryside, grandparents; people would
regularly go to their grandparents and come back with eggs
and meat. There was food. It's not like we were starving.
And if you paid a little more, you could buy semi-prepared
food, kind of like Trader Joe's-type stuff here. It was a
little more expensive, but it was available. For example,
semi-prepared schnitzels.

My dad would buy them because he was well paid; he had a
good salary as a professor. Most people would not dream of
buying that on a regular basis.

So we had food. I can't say we had a horrible life. We
didn't. And the propaganda would not really affect you on a
day-to-day basis. You would mostly be making fun among
yourselves at home, like, "Oh, look, they are singing
praises to this guy," and we were like examining to see —
okay, is he sick? Is he likely to die soon? What's
happening? It was kind of a laughingstock at some point, in
the last years.

Ileana: So what led up to the whole 1989 revolution? Was it
mostly the fact that Germany had already fallen, or was
there some particular thing?

George: So what happened was that the Soviet Union kind of
opened up a little bit, with glasnost and Gorbachev, and
they let go a little bit of the grip on the communist bloc.
There were various changes in Czechoslovakia and other
countries around us in the Warsaw Pact; one by one they were
falling and changing regime. That couldn't be kept secret,
because Radio Free Europe was blasting everything that was
happening, so everybody knew what was going on. The
movements in Romania for 1989 started in the western part,
in Timișoara. People actually got out on the streets, and
that was kept hush-hush. Of course, on TV they were saying
that there were some groups of hooligans going out on the
street and they were promptly reprimanded and brought to
order. But we could hear on Radio Free Europe that it was
more than that. And so, slowly, people started going out in
the streets in other cities as well, and Ceaușescu convened
a large gathering, a meeting.

He came out on the balcony and started to tell people he was
going to increase pay, he was going to give everybody 100
lei extra a month or something like that, and people started
booing. People started booing.

I don't know if there were agitators in the crowd or not,
but people started booing and throwing the pictures with the
leaders on the floor, and Ceaușescu could not believe his
eyes. He started to scream, "Hello, hello, stop, keep it
quiet, people, what's going on?" He could not believe people
were booing.

Ileana: So you think he genuinely didn't believe that people
didn't like him?

George: He was surrounded by yes-men, and he was always told
that he was the most beloved person in the whole country.

I'm not sure how much he knew how much he was hated.
Honestly, I don't know how much. But suffice it to say that
when he saw what was going on, he got his wife, and they got
into that helicopter and took off.

They wanted to flee. A few hours later — I don't even know —
a few hours later, their pilot betrayed them and landed
somewhere on a military base, and they were arrested.

Ileana: How come they were arrested?

George: Well, they were arrested because people were already
in the streets, chanting and saying that they had won,
because the dictator had fled. And on TV — immediately on TV
— people showed up who were known anti-communist
intellectuals, well-known people who had been spoken about
on Radio Free Europe, who had been opposing the regime. They
came on TV, and everybody knew who they were. They told
people, "Hey guys, brothers, we have won, the dictator has
fled, and come and defend the television station, because we
don't want him to come back. If he comes back, he's going to
kill all of us." And people did. They went there, and
somehow there was some kind of ambush, misunderstandings —
they got killed. People were shooting at each other. I don't
know if it was the military with the secret police shooting
at each other, or military shooting at military. Nobody knew
who the enemy was, but civilians actually got shot and
killed.

Ileana: But civilians — how would they have had guns?

George: They didn't have guns, exactly. So it must have been
military shooting. Civilians just went there, at some point
— not immediately, but at some point. I think the military,
the army, signaled that they were on the side of the
revolutionaries. We had some generals come on TV and say,
"Okay, the army is with us." And I do believe that the
military handed out some guns to civilians to protect
themselves and to protect the institutions of the state.

So some civilians got hold of some weapons, for sure. I
don't know who shot whom; nobody knows, because it was never
resolved who killed whom and how. But cars got set on fire,
buildings got set on fire, windows got broken, people got
shot.

I could see the holes on the walls in many buildings in the
city, and for four days dead people were in the street until
they got burned and charred — charred corpses.

Yeah, no, it was a complete disaster.

Ileana: Well, okay, so what did you do in that aftermath?

George: Well, first of all, let me walk you through how that
day was for me, and then the aftermath. I wasn't in
Bucharest, I was in another city, just vacationing actually.
We saw people starting to march on the street — in the
middle of the street, large groups of workers, actually,
from factories, chanting and telling people to join them.
They were fighting for bread — they were just chanting
"bread, bread, we need bread," not "freedom" — bread.
Freedom was too abstract; they were honestly concerned with
survival, chanting "cowards, cowards, come and join us."

And some people joined them.

I was taking pictures, taking pictures to document what was
happening.

I saw the military getting deployed around City Hall. There
were soldiers with their Kalashnikovs pointed upward,
defending the City Hall, and I could see they were terrified
too, because they knew they could be asked to shoot at the
crowd.

I didn't stay to see what was happening, because I started
hearing gunshots, and I had been in the army, and I knew
those guns were for real and you could die. I don't know if
they were shooting in the air or if they shot at people in
that city. I have no idea.

Anyway, when I got inside — actually, as I was walking, I
saw people on their own balconies chanting that we had won,
and they were propping their TVs facing outward, so that
people could see that there were people on TV telling us the
dictator had fled. So I could see what was happening. Before
that, I didn't know what the point of marching in a city
that was not the capital would be. Like, what's the point of
that? Because still, nothing could be changed just from
people marching in a random town. I did not know that this
was happening all over the country, but then I did. So the
next day there were newspapers that already, on the front
page, were talking about the dictator fleeing and that we
had won.

There were free newspapers — there were just children
distributing them, like in the movies, handing out
newspapers for free.

It was surreal, because all my life all I knew was the
communist regime, tight-lipped and always being fed the
party line, and seeing newspapers like that was completely
surreal.

The first pictures they had in those newspapers were from
the compound where the dictator lived, showing us the kind
of luxury he was living in — the gold stuff and furniture —
completely obnoxious living, just to show us the contrast
between everybody's day-to-day meager existence and how
these dictators lived. But at this point, they were not yet
arrested. They got arrested — we heard that they were
arrested. We didn't actually know whether they were or not,
only two days later. Meanwhile, during these two days, there
were still people being shot — by whom, nobody knows. There
were rumors that there were foreign mercenaries, essentially
— we were calling them terrorists — that were somehow hired
by the dictator to defend him, and they continued to fight
until it was clear that he was captured. Then there was a
kind of trial, but it was a joke of a trial.

Ileana: Yeah, I've seen the trial.

George: Yeah, and they got shot immediately after the trial.

Ileana: Yeah, so any other... no, no, I think with...

George: Okay, so the aftermath. So now they got shot. It was
basically right after Christmas, or on Christmas Day — they
got shot. I think it was December 25. And then I got back to
Bucharest, and I could not recognize my own city. I could
not recognize it. There were bullet holes everywhere in the
apartment buildings, broken windows, like I said, charred
corpses on the streets, checkpoints everywhere checking if
you were on the side of the revolution or not. I don't know
how they could tell, but anyway.

My dad told me to go and defend the university. I went, but
I didn't really see much for me to do, because there were a
bunch of people with guns — civilians with guns. What are
civilians doing with guns? So I took off, I didn't stay.

A few days later, the first private stores started opening
up. That's quick — very quick. Within days, there were shops
springing up, and they were private, and you could not buy
anything with the national currency. Everything was sold
only for hard currency, dollars — only dollars, or Western
money. Why? Because the inflation was rampant. From the
morning to the evening, the national currency was losing
value, so they couldn't keep up with changing the prices.

Ileana: So why exactly was the currency losing value?

George: I'm not completely sure how that happened. I really
don't know, but it was happening right before your eyes.
There was merchandise available that was probably smuggled
immediately over the border, people had money — they had
money saved up or whatever — and they were just walking in
to spend the money.

I don't know how, but immediately after that, salaries
started increasing because prices were going up, so it was
like a cat-and-mouse race. The salaries, even the official
state-paid salaries, were increasing to keep up with the
cost of living, which would make the cost of living even
higher, every week, to the point that — I don't remember how
long after — you could buy a Coca-Cola bottle for 1,000 lei.
Now, 1,000 lei was worth more like $1 at some point. So that
1,000 lei — the salary just before '89 was 2,000 a month.
Now 1,000 would buy you a Coca-Cola, 2,000 a loaf of bread,
right? So it was just going crazy like that. At some point
the government stepped in and said, "Okay, overnight we're
going to change the currency, and 10,000 of the old lei will
be one new lei." 10,000 to one — that's what it settled on.
And then it stayed relatively stable after that. Nowadays
there are like four or five new lei to the dollar. Before,
it was like 50,000 — something crazy. I was at the time
employed at the university; I had an assistant professor
type of job, and our salary was in the tens of thousands of
lei, to the point that they didn't actually have banknotes
to pay us with. They had to take them from a batch of notes
that was going to be melted and recycled.

Ileana: How could you know?

George: Because they had sand on them. They had sand on them
— they were preparing them to be burned. That's what they
were paying us with — big, big stashes of money, which was
almost worthless. But that was the salary. That's how the
salary was paid.

And yeah, if you wanted to buy anything, you needed to have
hard currency, and some people had it; I didn't. You could
buy it at very bad exchange rates. There were exchange
offices sprouting up immediately in the city, all over the
place. On every street corner, there was a currency
exchange.

Ileana: What else? I feel like I was going to ask something.
Were you, like, hopeful at all? Did you feel any sense of
revolution?

George: Yes. Extremely hopeful that things were going to
improve. Extremely hopeful that with freedom — freedom of
expression and freedom of speech — would also come
prosperity, right away. We were extremely hopeful that the
country would open up to exchange of ideas with the West.

We were students at the time, so everybody was hoping that
we would be able to go on exchange programs and spend some
time in the West and come back, maybe get some kind of
degree in the West and come back and work in Romania.
Everybody was very hopeful for that.

None of that happened.

What actually happened was that sooner or later a lot of
people of my generation figured out that things were not
going to improve, and that the old communist ruling class
was still in power, with rebranded political affiliations.
There was something like the Social Democrat Party, which
was basically still the same old communists. The problem was
